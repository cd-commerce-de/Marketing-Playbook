Marketing Playbook

The working manual for everything that drives visibility, conversion, and revenue across our brand portfolio. Use this as your style guide, best-practice checklist, and shared source of truth.

Internal Document Marketing & Brand Management Confidential

This playbook exists to make ownership of marketing live inside the team that does the work. Every section you read below was pulled from what we actually do — captured from working sessions, refined, and written as instructions you can follow, challenge, and improve.

Use each section as a mini style guide for that activity. If you are running BAU brand management, start there. If you are launching a product, go to Launch. If you are building a deal plan, Deals is your chapter. Every section ends with a short checklist so you can verify work before it goes live.

If something here is wrong, outdated, or missing, flag it. The worst mistake is treating this document as final — it should evolve as fast as our strategies do.

Playbook style rules

Profit issue — check every driver, not just one

A profit miss is almost never one cause. Go through the full list every time. Don't stop at the first hit.

Profit diagnostic checklist

  • Refunds — did a spike kick in this week?
  • Amazon fees — any fee changes, category shift, dimension recalculation?
  • PPC / TACOS — any campaign running hot?
  • COGS percentage — if running deals, rising COGS % can be normal. Rule: the rise in COGS % should roughly match the deal discount. If much higher, investigate.
  • Review score drop — impacts conversion and organic ranking
  • Returns — check long-term return rate for the "frequently returned" badge risk (keep under 4–5%)

Revenue issue — the diagnostic order matters

1

Is the ASIN active?

First thing to check. Suppressed, blocked, or compliance issue will kill everything downstream. If blocked or unblocked: hand to Account Management — it's a different process.

2

Is the listing active or suppressed?

Listing suppression is different from ASIN block. Same tool, different cause.

3

Has the Browse Node changed?

If the listing was moved to the wrong category, relevant keywords stop ranking. This is commonly missed — always check.

4

Out of stock?

If yes: inform Head of Marketing, discuss with Planner. If no: continue.

5

Are the key listing assets stable?

Title, images, bullets, A+ content. Did someone make a change? Diff against last known good.

6

Check market demand

Use SQP data and tools like Sellersprite / Datarova. If category demand is down year-on-year, the revenue drop may be demand-driven, not execution-driven.

7

Check impressions, then conversion rate

If impressions are down — it's a visibility problem (ranking, ads). If impressions are fine but CVR dropped — it's a listing / price / image problem.

The CEO-of-the-brand mindset

You own the brand. Not the supply chain team. Not the launch team. You. When you see a top seller going out of stock, you have permission to escalate loudly — not politely. When you see a product with poor margin and suspect the price strategy is wrong, run a lean thesis-and-test: stake a hypothesis, test for two to four weeks, reverse if wrong.

Frame every bet as a one-way door or two-way door. Two-way: try it. One-way (e.g. merging parent ASINs, new marketplace expansion): slow down, measure consequences first. We got burnt before by merging parents "to simplify PPC" and not monitoring closely — a year later the impression drop showed up. Don't repeat that.

BAU Brand Management — style rules

  • When performance drops, always start at the flowchart. Don't jump to PPC as the default fix.
  • When performance exceeds, check stock first. Exceeding plan on a low-stock SKU is worse than missing it.
  • Profit diagnosis: check every driver, never stop at the first hit.
  • Revenue diagnosis: check in the exact order — ASIN → listing → browse node → stock → assets → demand → impressions → CVR.
  • Demand trend must be checked before blaming execution. If category demand is down, the miss may not be yours.
Section 03 — Launch

Launching a new product

The launch window is short and unforgiving. This chapter codifies the pre-launch, launch week, and post-launch steps that protect the honeymoon period and set up long-term ranking.

The pre-launch price setup — non-negotiable

This is the single most important launch-related habit. It determines whether we can ever run real deals on this SKU.

Pre-launch price rules

  • Launch at the intended list price, not the intended sale price. The validated reference price (VRP) is what Amazon approves as "list price"; we only get it if we actually sold at that price for several weeks.
  • Wait a few weeks before running any deal or price discount. Let Amazon accept the reference price first.
  • Never change the basis price manually from Manage All Inventory — use price discounts, coupons, and deals only. Manual changes kill the VRP.
  • Once VRP is established, open promotions in this order: coupon (to cushion price shock), then price discount, then deal.

Old approach

Launch at target sale price, don't actively manage list price. Result: VRP never validated, deals get rejected, strike prices look weak.

Current approach

Launch at elevated VRP, hold, then activate coupon → price discount → deal. Protects long-term deal pricing and strike prices.

The launch workflow

1

R&D hands off keyword research

The keyword sheet (see Keyword Research chapter) arrives with the product. Review it — don't blindly trust it, especially if you are native-speaking the market.

2

Listing assets built

Title, bullets, Q&A, description, images, A+ content. The new process is AI-assisted with human review — see SEO & Copy chapter. Native-speaker review is required before publish.

3

Campaigns prepared in drafts

Brand Manager drafts the campaigns ahead of go-live. At launch, only the ASIN/SKU needs to be plugged in. The Ads Specialist / Head of Marketing handles optimisation in the first weeks.

4

FBA vs FBM decision

Never default. FBA gives the Prime badge, which materially lifts conversion in Germany. But FBA fees on bulky items can erode margin to zero. Calculate both, bring the recommendation to Head of Marketing.

5

Launch day — visibility over efficiency

Bids set 20% above the suggested max to guarantee impressions. Budget at least €50/day for exact-match, €20/day for auto/broad. Goal is page-one visibility fast, not ACOS.

6

Honeymoon period — daily touches

Ignore the usual "don't change PPC daily" rule. During launch, make daily changes to capitalise on Amazon's new-product algorithm boost. Watch budget burn — products can still run out of budget despite high limits.

7

Post-launch SEO audit (3+ days after copy submission)

Verify the listing displays as intended, A+ content rendered, no attribute issues. Then run the SEO audit prompt (see SEO chapter) to confirm all critical Q&A points are addressed somewhere in the listing.

8

Keyword revisit date set

When the launch is approved, the Launch Manager provides the go-live date. The SEO team calculates when to revisit keywords based on performance. Put the revisit on the calendar at launch — don't rely on remembering.

Launch campaign structure (lean by design)

We deliberately run a lean launch structure. Less is more — we don't need to target every long-tail keyword. Ranking for main keywords pulls long-tail ranking along.

Single-keyword campaigns
Exact match

Top 2–5 keywords, one per campaign. Fixed bid, high enough to get impressions fast.

Top keywords campaign
Broad match

All top keywords together in one broad campaign.

Top competitors campaign
ASIN targeting

Top revenue competitors only — don't target the whole field.

Auto campaign
All match types

Close match, substitutes, complements, loose match — all on during launch.

Old launch structure

Fat-head, main, size, material, competitor, category targeting, self-protection — up to 15+ campaigns per product. High maintenance, many weak signals.

Current launch structure

4 campaign types, usually <10 campaigns total. Target what drives sales; trust organic pickup for long-tail once main keywords rank.

FBA vs FBM — the calculation you have to run

This is a one-way door if you commit large stock. Always calculate before launch, especially outside Germany where Prime may not apply.

FBA/FBM decision inputs

  • Product dimensions — standard size unlocks the lowest fee tier
  • Marketplace — Prime badge impact is huge in Germany; smaller in FR/IT/ES
  • Category fee differential
  • Competitor fulfilment pattern — if the top 10 are all Prime, you need Prime
  • FBA multi-country enrollment bonus — being active in DE + IT + ES + FR + NL gives a fee reduction across all markets. Losing one kills the reduction for all.
Launch in France / Italy / Spain without a Prime badge is often a structural disadvantage. If FBA is feasible at reasonable fees, default to FBA for the main market launch, even if it means delaying by a few weeks while stock ships into Amazon.

Launch — style rules

  • VRP first, deals second. Never run a promotion in the first 2–3 weeks.
  • Visibility beats ACOS during the honeymoon. Budget and bids high, daily adjustments allowed.
  • Lean campaign structure. 4 types, under 10 campaigns per product.
  • Calculate FBA vs FBM before launch, never after.
  • Schedule the keyword revisit date on day one. Don't trust memory.
  • Native-speaker reviews every listing before publish, even when AI drafted it.
Section 04 — Deals & Pricing

Deals, coupons, price discounts

Deals are our biggest weekly revenue lever and our biggest pricing risk. This chapter covers the rules, the four strategies, and the weekly submission workflow.

The foundation: what the VRP is and why it dominates everything

VRP = Validated Reference Price. The list price Amazon has actually approved as legitimate for this SKU — based on a history of us selling at that price. Without a VRP, most deal types are rejected.

If we damage the VRP, our strike price disappears, our "was" price disappears, and the product looks like it's at normal price with no discount. That is the single worst pricing failure we can have.

The only safe way to manage price

Pricing non-negotiables

  • Never change the base price manually in Manage All Inventory — it nukes the VRP.
  • Always work through coupons, price discounts, and deals.
  • Deal price must always be the lowest of all active promotions. If coupon is lower than deal, you have to beat the coupon price on the next deal — each cycle drives the floor down.
  • Never let a coupon and a deal overlap on the same SKU. The lowest combined price becomes the new 30-day low and kills future deal pricing.
  • Schedule coupons to end at least one hour before deals start.

Pricing rule cheat sheet

RuleApplies toWhat it means
Minimum 15% off VRPDeals (DE, UK, NL, IE)Biggest discount you must give. Without VRP, deal is rejected.
Minimum 5% off sales-history ("was") priceDealsAmazon uses 90-day history; if you recently sold below VRP, that becomes the reference you must beat.
Minimum 5% off current priceDeals, price discountsAgainst the live price including active price discounts. Coupon not counted here.
Match/beat lowest price in last 30 daysAll promotion types — coupons includedThis is the only rule that includes coupons. Key reason we schedule coupons never to overlap deals.
Omnibus rule: 5% off lowest 30-day priceFR, IT, ES (and coming to DE)Strike price = lowest 30-day price, not VRP. Every deal cycle lowers the base by 5%. Plan accordingly.
Omnibus outlook: The rule is coming to Germany — no confirmed timeline, but assume "any week." When it lands, we cannot run deals every two weeks on the same SKU anymore. Strategy 3 (weekly deals via color rotation) is the one most exposed. Start testing alternatives now.

The four deal strategies

We run four strategies across the portfolio. Each brand / product line uses the strategy that fits its economics, seasonality, and marketplace rules. The Deals Specialist assigns strategies; Brand Managers execute.

Strategy 1
Every 4 weeks

Deal with cooldown, no coupon during cooldown.

Omnibus-compliant in principle (needs tweak to 30 days).

Smaller / lower-price products, hitches, many PD SKUs
Strategy 2
Rotate variants weekly

Deal on one variant while siblings get coupons. Listing shows a deal every single week.

Omnibus-compliant in spirit.

Multi-colour / multi-variant SKUs
Strategy 3
2 weeks on / 2 off

Aggressive deal cadence for SKUs without enough variants for Strategy 2.

Not Omnibus-compatible.

Trailer tarpaulins, pool covers
Strategy 4
Flexible + event-driven

Mix of deals, price discounts, coupons. Reserves cooldown before Prime Day / BFCM so SKU is eligible.

Seasonal / event-heavy / high-attention products
Strategy 4 rule: To put an ASIN into a major deal event, the cooldown before the event must be at least 40 days. Plan event participation backward from the event date.

The weekly deal submission workflow

1

Plan 3 cycles ahead (≈1.5 months)

Each Brand Manager prepares submission files covering the next three two-week cycles. One file per cycle — never mix dates in a single file.

2

Schedule coupons for cooldown weeks upfront

At the same time you schedule deals, schedule coupons for the weeks between deals. Makes overlap mistakes nearly impossible and ensures the listing never goes naked.

3

Name each file correctly

Include brand, year, and calendar week range. "NW_2025_CW22-23.xlsx" — not "deals final v3.xlsx". This is a non-negotiable.

4

Submit by Thursday cut-off to the Deals Specialist

The Deals Specialist consolidates all BMs' lists into one master file and sends to Amazon's Deals Manager. One consolidated list per cycle — much cleaner for rejection tracking.

5

Always submit early for multiple resubmission attempts

Submit 2 weeks ahead of the deal start, not 1 week. Rejections need re-work time. Submitting late gives you one shot.

6

Thursday = validation day

Validation list arrives Thursdays. Review rejections same-day; submit corrections the same day to maximise re-submission attempts.

7

Handle rejections by cause

"No reference price" — usually a low-velocity variant; resubmit only if it matters. "Does not meet discount vs VRP" — Amazon adjusted the VRP; resubmit with new price or +1 cent to pass validation. Document every case in the deal tracker.

8

Use the Deals Manager's VRP preview (Mondays)

The Deals Manager sends a weekly VRP & recommended-deal list. Compare against what we planned — reveals VRP changes before we submit. Consider building a history view that flags VRP movements.

Price Discount specifics

Price discount operating rules

  • Always submit <= 30 days.
  • You can lower the discount after submission but you cannot raise it — submit conservative first.
  • Description must include the SKU so you can search it later. Internal-only field, but discipline pays off at scale.
  • Audience type: "All customers" by default. Prime Exclusive only makes sense during deal events (for the event-equivalent badge).
  • Starts six hours after submission; during those six hours, you can still edit.
  • If a deal falls inside an active price discount window, Amazon suppresses the price discount for that period — this is normal.

Coupon specifics

Coupon operating rules

  • For low-price products: use money-off (customer doesn't have to calculate). For higher-price products: use percentage (more visible).
  • Set budget high (e.g. 10,000) so it never cuts off mid-campaign.
  • Coupon title must be in the market's language — use existing coupons as copy-paste templates. Don't invent new wording on the fly.
  • Stacking with price discount: Amazon applies only the higher benefit, not both. Don't rely on stacking.
  • Check daily that every active listing still has its coupon/deal badge displayed. If a badge goes missing, investigate via Advertising → Price Discounts immediately.

When the deal fails to display

You see a low price but no badge, no strike-through. This is one of the most damaging silent failures. Diagnose fast:

1

Confirm VRP is gone via Price Discounts tool

Create (don't submit) a dummy price discount for the ASIN. The tool shows "no reference price" if VRP is lost. Cancel the draft.

2

Push the price back up immediately

Cancel the active price discount and/or pause the deal. Raise the listing price back toward target VRP.

3

Use a high coupon to hold net price

Coupons don't affect VRP. Raise the base price, stack a large coupon so customer net price is unchanged. Observe daily for VRP recovery.

4

Re-enable deal once VRP returns

No fixed timeline — can be 1 week or 1 month. Check daily with the dummy-price-discount trick.

Deals — style rules

  • The deal price is always the lowest of any active promo.
  • Never overlap a coupon with a deal. End coupons an hour before deals start.
  • Work three cycles ahead, one file per cycle, named with calendar week.
  • Submit two weeks ahead of start — not one — for rejection re-submission buffer.
  • Track coupons and deals in the deal tracker — no active promo should be invisible to the team.
  • When a deal badge goes missing, treat it as a P1 incident — fix before the end of the day.
Section 05 — SEO & Copy

Writing listings that rank and convert

The new process is AI-assisted, review-heavy, and split across SEO keyword owner and native-speaker copywriter. This chapter tells you how both sides work and how to stitch them together.

The new copy process — what changed

Old approach

Native-speaker copywriter drafts the title, bullets, Q&A, description in one pass. Keywords added by feel, not by scoring. Review was informal.

  • Intuitive keyword placement
  • Strong readability
  • Low signal on keyword coverage

Current approach

SEO lead prepares the keyword sheet with scoring (demand, revenue, CVR, relevancy, final score). Native-speaker copywriter drafts raw readable copy. SEO lead then layers in the priority keywords. Final review by native speaker before publish.

  • Explicit keyword scoring per asset position
  • Readability preserved via native-speaker raw draft
  • Stronger competitor-review integration via AI
Critical gap to watch: An AI-written listing in a language the writer doesn't speak natively will miss subtle nuances — pluralisation, compound-word rules, idiomatic phrasing. Native-speaker review is the non-negotiable gate. No listing ships without it.

The copy creation workflow

1

Marketing analysis sheet (AI-assisted)

Pull positive and negative reviews from Product Opportunity Explorer. Use these as raw input for the ChatGPT customer-sentiment project.

2

Competitor analysis (5+ competitors)

Pick at least five direct competitors (main / top in the niche). Download reviews for each competitor ASIN via Sellersprite. Also download reviews of our own ASIN if it exists.

3

Run customer-sentiment analysis

In the dedicated ChatGPT project (Customer Sentiment Analysis), run the prompt over our reviews + competitor reviews. Output: customer benefits, competitor weak points, heavily promoted features, prioritised features, emotional selling points.

4

Verify our product actually solves the weak points

Before adding a "solves X" claim to the listing, confirm our product solves X. Some complaints are category-wide (e.g. inflatable products lose air over time) — don't over-promise.

5

Title — SEO lead, then native-speaker review

Format: brand + main keyword + key feature keyword + material keyword. Highlight every used keyword in bold in the working doc. Do not repeat the same single word more than twice in the title — Amazon will suppress.

6

Bullet points — native-speaker raw draft, SEO lead adds keywords

Each bullet covers one core feature/benefit. Blend review-driven language with selling points. Do not rewrite bullets every quarter — tune keywords and seasonal terms only (Christmas, Easter) unless the product itself changes.

7

Q&A block

Use the customer-sentiment output to structure real questions. Address unanswered questions (what's in the box, etc.) in images first, then bullets.

8

Description

Combine lifestyle storytelling, emotional connection, and daily-use functionality. Use the same sentiment project and add remaining keywords via Frankenstein / scribbles.

9

Keep the working doc bilingual

Column A: DE copy. Column B: EN translation. This unblocks Launch Manager, PPC, and leadership review — no manual translation loops.

10

Post-launch SEO audit — 3+ days after copy submission

Wait for the product detail page to refresh, then run the audit prompt. Verify every critical question is answered somewhere in the listing (title / bullets / Q&A / description / images).

Title rules — Amazon suppression triggers

Title must-follow rules

  • Max 2 repetitions of the same single word. Three is suppressed.
  • Singular + plural count as different forms (acceptable to use both).
  • Different spellings / spacing (e.g. "Dartboard" vs "Dart Board") count as different keywords — legal.
  • No registered ® symbol in the title if used in A+ content comparison tables in FR — it breaks A+ upload.
  • Title format: Brand → Main Keyword → Key Feature + Keyword → Material + Keyword.

Image & A+ content guidance

Image and A+ guidance

  • Include what's-in-the-box imagery — questions about package contents are usually image-solved first, text second.
  • Include add-on / complementary product imagery — most of our listings have an add-on value proposition.
  • Lifestyle infographics should reflect the top 5 themes from the customer-sentiment analysis.
  • Comparison tables in A+: avoid registered symbols in product headlines/titles — triggers upload rejection in some markets.
  • In FR marketplace specifically, A+ comparison tables sometimes reject product names pulled from Germany — if the upload fails, publish without the comparison table first, then flag for resolution.

Post-launch SEO audit — the prompt

Three days after the listing goes live, use the structured audit prompt (stored in the working doc). Inputs: title, bullets, description, Q&A. The audit asks whether each critical Q&A point is addressed in the listing — yes/no/partial. "No" items get added to the bullets or images in the next revision.

SEO & Copy — style rules

  • Native-speaker review is the last gate before publish. Always.
  • Never over-promise. Only claim what the product actually delivers — even if competitors' reviews complain about it.
  • Max 2 reps of a word in the title. Check before submit.
  • Keep the working doc bilingual (DE/EN side by side).
  • Post-launch audit runs 3 days after the listing updates, not before.
  • Don't rewrite bullets every quarter — tune keywords only unless the product changed.
Section 06 — Keyword Research

Multi-tool scoring, competitor analysis, prioritisation

Keyword research is the input to both SEO copy and PPC. The scoring model unifies signals from five tools so we target what actually drives sales — not just what has search volume.

The tool stack

The scoring model

Each keyword gets four sub-scores, then a final score. Each sub-score is computed by comparing the keyword's value against every other keyword in the sheet, converting to a percentile, then averaging across tools.

Demand
How many people search this keyword
Search volume across tools
Revenue
How much money this keyword drives
Purchase volume × price
Conversion
How well searchers buy
Purchases / searches
Relevancy
How closely the keyword matches the niche
Subjective + tool-reported relevancy
Final score
Overall keyword strength
Blended priority signal
Key insight: High search volume with low conversion is a trap — you'll burn ad spend and the R&D team may misread demand. The scoring model exists so we don't rank niche size on volume alone.

Keyword sheet workflow

1

List top competitors

First tab of the keyword sheet: 2–5 direct competitors for the product. Pulled from POE / Sellersprite / manual Amazon search.

2

Pull keyword data from each tool

One tab per tool. Raw exports — don't clean prematurely. You lose signal.

3

Build the keyword summary

Consolidated tab: keyword, per-tool demand/revenue/CVR/relevancy, final score.

4

Filter for language and intent

For German keywords: automated translation often looks close but isn't relevant. Head of Marketing reviews and marks which keywords are actually connected to the product. This step cannot be skipped for DE.

5

Segment by intent

Not by alphabet. Group keywords by buyer intent: generic category → specific feature → size/capacity → color/variant → English-version (yes, Germans search English terms for some categories). Segmenting drives the PPC campaign structure later.

6

Assign keyword to asset position

Top keywords by score → title. Next tier → bullets (highlighted bold in the doc). Remaining → description. Unused → back-end search terms.

7

Feed the PPC plan from the same sheet

The sheet is dual-purpose: SEO copy input AND PPC campaign planning input. Same top keywords become single-keyword exact campaigns.

Revisit triggers

  • Scheduled revisit date set on launch (see Launch chapter) — based on expected post-launch data horizon.
  • Unexpected strong performance on a previously-ignored keyword (discovered via search-term report).
  • Major market event (new competitor launch, category name change, seasonal demand shift).
  • Annual baseline refresh for top revenue SKUs, even if they're stable.

Section 07 — PPC / Amazon Ads

Campaigns, bids, optimisation

We run a deliberately lean structure. Fewer campaigns, better data, less maintenance. This chapter covers the structure, the naming convention, and the weekly optimisation rhythm.

The lean campaign philosophy

Old structure (15+ campaigns per product)

Fat-head, main, size, material, competitor, category targeting, self-protection, multi-match-type campaigns with ad groups per match type.

  • High maintenance
  • Many weak-signal campaigns
  • Long-tail targeting drove up exact-match CPC across the market

Current structure (<10 campaigns per product)

Single-keyword exact + top-keywords broad + top-competitor ASIN + auto (all match types). Separate broad campaign for keywords not getting impressions in multi-match campaigns.

  • Simpler, faster optimisation
  • TACOS stays healthy, sometimes better than larger accounts
  • Long-tail still ranks organically, pulled up by main-keyword velocity
Core learning: You don't need to target every keyword. Organic ranking on long-tail follows from exact-match velocity on main keywords. We target <5 single-keyword exact campaigns per product and trust the algorithm for the rest.

Bidding strategy

Past default: Fixed bids

Worked for our account when we ran more campaigns. Predictable, easy to optimise, lower risk of bid explosion.

Current default: Fixed bids on launch, Dynamic down-only post-launch

Fixed bids initially for impression speed. Once campaigns have data and TACOS is stable, switch to dynamic down-only to reduce waste on low-conversion-likelihood impressions. Up-and-down only used for catch-all with very low bids — Amazon can double, but doubled low-bid is still low.

Bulk optimisation approach

We do bulk optimisations inside the advertising console, not through third-party tools. Some accounts of our size use Perpetua, Scale Insights, Adbadger, Intentwise — we've deliberately stayed in-console.

Bulk optimisation rules

  • Never change PPC every day (except during launch). The attribution window means data takes 48h to stabilise.
  • Wednesday is the bulk day — gives Monday/Tuesday data time to settle.
  • Filter by portfolio before making changes — don't operate on all products at once.
  • For catch-all campaigns, be extra cautious: the low-bid limits get multiplied by up-and-down bidding. Check every bid change doesn't exceed the catch-all ceiling.

Section 08 — E-commerce (eBay & DTC)

Non-Amazon channels

eBay and our own online shops are smaller in volume but strategically important — for price flexibility, brand building, and reducing Amazon dependency. Price coordination across channels is the single biggest operational risk.

The buy-box cross-channel risk

Critical rule: Amazon actively scans other platforms for pricing. If we sell a product cheaper on eBay or our own shop than on Amazon, Amazon can suppress the Amazon buy-box until we match. Coordinate every price change across channels.

Price change coordination

Coordination rules

  • Before raising a price on the online shop, check Amazon deal / coupon / price-discount activity for the same SKU. A raise on DTC while Amazon runs a deal is safe; a raise on DTC while Amazon is at VRP can distort Amazon's reference.
  • Before lowering a price on eBay or DTC, check Amazon's buy-box status. Going lower than Amazon risks losing the Amazon buy-box.
  • When the E-commerce Manager raises shop prices, expect short-term unit volume dips. This is intentional — margin protection, not a performance miss.

Observed cross-channel pattern

Recent experience: shop price increases produced lower unit sales but higher absolute profit (e.g. an inflatable mattress line: unit volume down 30%, profit up €400). When the price increase was defensive (protecting margin), the trade paid off. When it was aggressive and the customer was price-sensitive, volume dropped without margin gain.

Rule of thumb: Run a price-change thesis for 2–4 weeks. Measure absolute profit, not units. If thesis fails, reverse.

eBay specifics

eBay operating rules

  • eBay is not a launch channel for new products. Amazon first — establish VRP, reviews, organic — then eBay.
  • Keep eBay prices at or above Amazon regular price. Do not undercut Amazon listing price except during authorised, coordinated promotions.
  • eBay imagery can differ slightly — lifestyle-heavy photos work well where Amazon imagery is infographic-heavy.
  • Customer service expectations differ — response time matters more on eBay; have a monitored mailbox.

DTC / own online shop

DTC operating rules

  • The shop is where we test pricing and bundles without impacting Amazon VRP.
  • Don't use shop deals to clear Amazon inventory — it's the wrong buyer segment.
  • Margin targets on DTC should be structurally higher than on Amazon (no 15% commission, no FBA fees).
  • Shop email list is a long-term asset — capture opt-in at every transaction. This is one of the few places where DTC can compound over time.

Brand-specific notes

BrandDTC / eBay note
Nasswerk, PlanenfuxPool season drives DTC spikes — schedule email marketing around May/June. eBay is steady.
TarpofixLower DTC penetration — buyers default to Amazon. Worth testing small trailer accessory bundles DTC-only.
HeimfleissCategory variation — composter buyers are DTC-friendly (long research cycle); fireplace-set buyers are Amazon-impulse.
PDBulk / B2B-tilt — DTC bulk-pricing page could unlock margin. Not in current scope but worth flagging.

E-commerce — style rules

  • Coordinate every price change across Amazon, eBay, and DTC.
  • Never undercut Amazon on eBay or DTC unless the campaign is explicitly cross-channel.
  • Measure profit, not units, when testing shop price changes.
  • DTC is for testing pricing and building the list, not Amazon inventory clearance.

The pick-up task form

When a Brand Manager needs an account-level action (listing fix, compliance question, IDQ correction), they submit a ClickUp form rather than messaging directly. Fields: task name, description, priority, brand, marketplace, ASIN, due date, type (images request, compliance, listing edit, etc.).

Why the form matters: The Account Health Specialist can triage by type and group similar tasks. Request-by-Slack leads to lost tasks and inconsistent turnaround. Always use the form.

Feedback removal workflow

1

Identify unjust feedback in Feedback Manager

Criteria: feedback about shipping speed (FBA fulfilled — our liability is minimal), product complaints that belong in reviews, off-topic comments.

2

Attempt removal via Amazon's in-tool option

First-pass automated removal. Works ~5% of the time.

3

Escalate via the primary case

4

Pan-EU FBA — protecting the fee reduction

Pan-EU protection routine

  • Most ineligibility is out-of-stock driven — escalate to Planner / Supply Chain immediately.
  • Some ineligibility is compliance-driven — case to Amazon, log in tracker.
  • Don't assume enrolment persists — re-confirm monthly via Manage FBA Inventory.

  • Pick-up tasks through the form, never direct Slack requests.
  • Weekly backup of category report — the only reliable rollback path.
  • Pan-EU eligibility is a cross-country risk — escalate fast when a country drops.
Section 10 — Processes

Cross-chapter workflows, step by step

These are the repeatable processes that cross chapter boundaries. Treat them as the definitive procedure — if you find yourself improvising, update the playbook.

Process 01 — Weekly deal submission (end to end)

1

Monday — pull VRP preview

Deals Manager sends weekly VRP & recommended-deal list. Each BM compares against planned deals. Adjust planned prices if VRP has moved.

2

Monday–Wednesday — prepare submission files

Each BM prepares one file per cycle (next three cycles). File naming: [Brand]_[Year]_CW[XX-YY].xlsx. Plan coupons for cooldown weeks at the same time.

3

Thursday cut-off — submit to Deals Specialist

Each BM sends files to the Deals Specialist. Deals Specialist consolidates into one master list per cycle.

4

Thursday/Friday — Deals Specialist submits to Amazon

Submits 2 weeks ahead of deal start, allowing room for rejection re-submissions.

5

Following Thursday — validation list arrives

Deals Specialist distributes per-BM rejections. Each BM reviews rejections same day.

6

Same Thursday — resubmit corrections

Use the resubmission tab (existing template). Send back to Deals Specialist, who forwards the consolidated re-sub list.

7

Deal start day (Monday) — walk the listings

Every BM verifies deal badges / strike prices / coupons display correctly for their SKUs. If any badge is missing, run the VRP-loss diagnostic (see Deals chapter) immediately.

Process 02 — New listing creation (AI-assisted)

1

SEO lead: pull competitor reviews

Sellersprite export for 5+ direct competitors plus our own ASIN if existing.

2

SEO lead: run customer-sentiment ChatGPT project

Inputs: product details, category, USPs/benefits, review files, current performance. Outputs: customer benefits, competitor weak points, heavily promoted features, prioritised features, emotional selling points.

3

SEO lead: validate solvability

For every weak point → confirm our product actually solves it. Drop anything we don't actually solve. No over-promising.

4

SEO lead: draft title

Format: brand + main keyword + key-feature keyword + material keyword. Highlight keywords in bold in the working doc. Check rep-count for every word.

5

Native-speaker copywriter: draft bullets, Q&A, description

Focus on readability, lifestyle, emotional connection. SEO lead adds priority keywords afterward.

6

Head of Marketing / native speaker: final review

Read full listing end-to-end. Look for: factual errors, awkward keyword insertions, Amazon-suppression risks (repetition, forbidden symbols).

7

Submit to listing system (Launch Manager)

DE + EN side by side in working doc. Image brief from customer-sentiment analysis feeds the creative team.

8

Wait 3+ days, then run post-launch SEO audit

Verify Q&A coverage, A+ rendering, attribute correctness. File any issues as pick-up tasks for the Account Health Specialist.

Process 03 — Performance drop diagnosis

1

Open the BM Decision Flowchart

Start with "plan met or not". Do not skip ahead.

2

Isolate profit vs revenue

Different diagnostic lists. Profit: check all drivers. Revenue: follow the ordered 7-step check in the BAU chapter.

3

Compare vs market demand before blaming ourselves

Pull SQP trend for the keyword set. If category demand is down, the revenue miss may be structural, not executional.

4

Walk the listing live

Open the Amazon product page as a customer. Badges displayed? Images loaded? A+ rendered? This catches silent failures the dashboard misses.

5

Cross-check return rate & reviews

A recent 1-star wave drops CVR invisibly. Check the last 14 days of reviews for a pattern.

6

Document in the weekly L10 notes

Hypothesis, evidence, planned action. Review in 1–2 weeks — thesis confirmed or reversed.

Process 04 — VRP recovery

When a deal/price-discount appears without a badge, or when you see a "no reference price" error on attempted submission, follow this process.

1

Confirm diagnosis

Open Advertising → Price Discounts → Create → add the ASIN. If it shows "no reference price", VRP is gone.

2

Cancel active low-price promotions immediately

Active price discount, active deal — pull them. They aren't helping and may be reinforcing the low VRP.

3

Raise the base price toward target VRP

Change the listing price in Manage All Inventory to the target VRP. This is the one exception to the "never change base manually" rule.

4

Stack a large coupon to hold customer net price

Coupons don't affect VRP. Customer net price is preserved; ranking and velocity protected.

5

Check VRP daily via the dummy-price-discount trick

Draft a dummy price discount each morning; check if reference price has returned. Cancel the draft.

6

When VRP returns — normal promos resume

Cancel the recovery coupon. Resume planned deal/price-discount schedule. Log the incident and cause in the deal tracker.

Process 05 — Weekly L10 prep (Brand Manager)

1

Monday morning: pull scorecard data

Sellerboard + P&L dashboard + SQP dashboard. Week-over-week and year-over-year for each brand you own.

2

Flag red items and pre-diagnose

For every red metric, have a one-line diagnosis ready. Not "TACOS is up" — "TACOS up 3pp because we pushed ads during launch; expected to normalise by week 4."

3

Identify actionable insights

For each brand, one insight that triggers action this week. Not just reporting — ownership.

4

Note cross-functional asks

Stock replenishment flags, supply chain questions, R&D feedback. Raise these explicitly in L10, don't save for later.

5

Prepare a micro-thesis for testing

Every L10 should produce one hypothesis to test in the next 2–4 weeks. "If we lower FP price by 5%, units will grow more than margin loses." Document expected outcome before the test.

1

Open the ClickUp pick-up task form

2

Fill every field

3
